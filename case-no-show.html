Predictive scoring system for medical appointment no-shows

Designed a binary classification and risk scoring framework to predict patient no-shows in a multi-site healthcare network, translating model probabilities into operational thresholds for overbooking, targeted reminders, and standard monitoring.

Healthcare Predictive modeling Operations analytics LightGBM Applied coursework

Context

Coursework project addressing a recurring operational challenge in healthcare networks: patient no-shows on scheduled appointments. The institutional context combined three impact dimensions that recur across healthcare organizations: financial (idle capacity of high-cost specialist time and infrastructure), social (blocked slots that could serve patients on waiting lists), and logistical (disrupted supply planning across specialties and locations).

Problem

Historical no-show rates created systemic inefficiency that institutional leadership could no longer absorb through manual planning. The challenge was to move from reactive scheduling to a data-driven, decision-informed operating model: estimate the probability of no-show for each scheduled appointment, translate that probability into actionable operational decisions, and avoid the methodological pitfalls (data leakage, class imbalance, accuracy-as-vanity-metric) that compromise this type of model in practice.

My role

Member of a four-person team. Contributed to problem framing, model selection strategy, scoring layer design, and translation of model outputs into operational thresholds and business rules.

Approach

  • Problem framing as propensity modeling: Formulated the task as supervised binary classification on tabular data, targeting the no-show outcome. Selected algorithm families appropriate for structured healthcare data (gradient boosting via LightGBM or XGBoost, with Random Forest as a robust alternative).
  • Multi-model benchmarking: Designed the evaluation to compare at least two algorithm families on honest metrics. Centered evaluation on ROC-AUC, precision, and recall rather than accuracy, given the expected class imbalance.
  • Class imbalance strategy: Defined a tiered approach starting with native model controls (class_weight='balanced') before resorting to sampling methods (SMOTE, undersampling, hybrid techniques), matching the technique to the severity of imbalance.
  • Data leakage prevention: Only features known before the appointment time were eligible for modeling. Engineered a "days of anticipation" feature from scheduling and appointment dates, capturing predictive lead time without contaminating the model with post-event information.
  • Scoring layer design: Translated model probabilities into a 0–100 risk score that operational staff could act on directly, avoiding the friction of communicating raw probabilities to non-technical stakeholders.
  • Hybrid scoring framework: Combined algorithmic outputs with expert business rules in a weighted scheme (model 70%, expert rules 30%).
  • Operational thresholds: Mapped score ranges to specific decisions: overbooking for high risk, targeted outreach for medium risk, standard monitoring for low risk.

Outcome

  • Complete methodological framework spanning model selection, evaluation, scoring layer, and operational integration.
  • Operational accountability: Each score band tied to a concrete action, making the model output auditable and actionable rather than abstract.
  • Defensible methodology: Explicit handling of class imbalance, data leakage, and metric selection — the exact failure modes that compromise predictive healthcare models in production.
  • Hybrid design respects institutional reality: Model-plus-rules architecture preserves clinical and operational expertise rather than replacing it, which is a precondition for adoption in healthcare settings.

Industrial relevance

No-show prediction is one of the highest-ROI use cases in healthcare analytics, and the methodology generalizes to any appointment-based service (clinics, dentistry, legal, financial advisory, government). The combination of predictive modeling, scoring layer design, and decision-band thresholds is the architecture used by mature operations analytics teams across industries.
