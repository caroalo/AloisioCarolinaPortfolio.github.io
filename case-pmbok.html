End-to-end project risk management framework for an ERP implementation

Applied the full PMBOK 7-process risk management framework to a realistic enterprise ERP rollout, including quantitative risk monetization, contingency reserve calculation, and integrated response planning.

Project management Risk analysis PMBOK ISO 9001 Applied coursework

Context

Coursework project applying the PMBOK risk management framework to a realistic enterprise scenario: the implementation of an integrated ERP system across production, purchasing, inventory, sales, and accounting functions of a mid-sized industrial manufacturer. Project scoped at nine months, with an approximate budget of 336M ARS, replacing a fragmented landscape of spreadsheets and isolated legacy systems.

Problem

ERP implementations are among the highest-risk IT projects in industrial settings. Failure modes are well-documented (resistance to change, scope creep, data migration issues, vendor delays, infrastructure gaps), and their consequences range from budget overruns to operational disruption of revenue-generating processes. The challenge was to apply a rigorous, auditable risk management methodology that would surface these risks early, quantify their financial impact, and define response strategies before execution.

My role

Project lead within a four-person team. Owned overall coordination, strategic decisions on methodology, and integration of outputs across the seven PMBOK processes.

Approach

Executed the complete PMBOK seven-process risk management cycle, complemented by ISO 9001:2015 risk-based thinking principles:

  • Risk management planning: Defined methodology, roles and responsibilities, evaluation criteria, and tooling (5×5 probability-impact matrix, Expected Monetary Value analysis, Gantt-based scheduling).
  • Risk identification: Surfaced 10 prioritized risks and 4 opportunities through stakeholder interviews, historical project review, and vendor engagement. Risks spanned organizational, technical, commercial, and operational dimensions.
  • Qualitative analysis: Scored each risk on probability and impact using the 5×5 matrix, classifying them as Critical, High, or Medium.
  • Quantitative analysis: Applied Expected Monetary Value methodology to monetize the top risks. Translated probability and impact into financial exposure. Defined a contingency reserve based on aggregated EMV of critical risks.
  • Response planning: Assigned an explicit PMBOK strategy (avoid, mitigate, transfer, accept, exploit, enhance) and concrete action to each risk. Strategies grouped across three intervention axes: technological, organizational, and people.
  • Response implementation: Defined ownership, sequencing, and integration of response actions across the project lifecycle.
  • Monitoring: Designed a continuous monitoring approach with feedback loops to surface emergent risks and feed them back into the analysis cycle, completing the closed-loop nature of the framework.

PMBOK cycle

Outcome

  • Complete deliverable: Risk register, 5×5 probability-impact matrix, EMV-based quantitative analysis, contingency reserve calculation, response plan, and Gantt-based project schedule.
  • Financial framing: Risk exposure expressed in monetary terms, supporting executive-level decision-making rather than abstract risk scoring.
  • Closed-loop design: Monitoring stage explicitly defined to feed new risks back into the identification process, demonstrating the iterative nature of project risk management.
  • Multi-standard integration: PMBOK methodology aligned with ISO 9001:2015 risk-based thinking, demonstrating awareness of how risk management connects to quality management systems in regulated environments.

Relevance to project management roles

This work demonstrates fluency with the PMBOK risk management framework end-to-end, including the quantitative side that is often skipped in practice. The combination of qualitative prioritization, monetary risk valuation, contingency reserve sizing, and integration with ISO quality standards is the toolkit expected from project and program managers in mid-to-large organizations, particularly in regulated or industrial settings where IT projects affect operational continuity.
