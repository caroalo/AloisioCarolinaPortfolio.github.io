Clinical information system gap analysis

Identified critical data structure and interface limitations blocking analytical use of the clinical information system, and delivered a prioritized recommendation that informed the institution's data quality roadmap.

Healthcare IT Systems analysis Requirements engineering Stakeholder communication Professional

Context

A multidisciplinary clinical committee at a tertiary care hospital was launching an analytics initiative to monitor antimicrobial prescription practices, multi-resistant organism prevalence, and patient outcomes. Effective execution depended on reliable access to clinical data through the hospital information system, which had been designed primarily for transactional clinical use rather than analytical reporting.

Problem

Initial data exploration revealed structural barriers that prevented the committee from operating with the data quality and granularity its analytical mandate required. Critical clinical information (deceased patient status, multi-resistant organism colonization, isolation indicators) was either embedded in free-text fields, encoded with ambiguous abbreviations, or not differentiated at the data model level. These limitations were invisible to clinical end users but blocked any systematic analytical use of the data and created downstream risks for data quality, operational efficiency, and clinical safety reporting.

My role

Acted as the bridge between the clinical committee and hospital IT. Owned the systems-level assessment, the negotiation of expanded data access, and the formal recommendation to medical and IT leadership.

Approach

  • Stakeholder coordination: Engaged hospital IT leadership directly to expand data access permissions from read-only to query-capable, enabling cross-table analysis previously not feasible.
  • System exploration and mapping: Documented the relational structure of the clinical information system, identifying how data flowed between patient records, medication orders, clinical events, and microbiology modules.
  • Coordinated reporting layer request: Worked with the hospital information system vendor's technical contact to scope a consolidated reporting view aligned with the analytical requirements of the stewardship program.
  • Gap identification: Catalogued specific interface and data model limitations that compromised analytical use, focusing on those with clinical safety implications.
  • Structured recommendation: Delivered a written recommendation to medical leadership prioritizing high-impact, low-effort interface improvements.
  • Documentation and handover: Maintained a written record of system limitations, access negotiations, and proposed changes to support follow-up and continuity.

Gap analysis

Outcome

  • Expanded analytical access to the clinical information system, enabling cross-table analysis that the committee could not previously perform.
  • Formal documentation of system limitations delivered to medical leadership, providing the institution with a prioritized roadmap of data quality improvements.
  • Improved data foundation for downstream stewardship analytics that previously would have required workarounds or manual extraction.
  • Established working channel between the clinical committee and hospital IT, supporting ongoing data quality discussions beyond the initial scope.

Industrial relevance

For healthcare organizations, regulated industries, and any setting where transactional systems are repurposed for analytics, this kind of gap analysis is where data initiatives succeed or fail. The work requires the combination of clinical or domain literacy, data modeling judgment, and the diplomatic skill to translate technical findings into recommendations that non-technical leadership will act on. Teams that lack this profile typically discover the gaps after committing to a reporting architecture, at significantly higher cost.
